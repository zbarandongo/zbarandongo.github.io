About Me

I am a research professional specializing in NGS Data Analysis with a particular focus on infectious diseases affecting wildlife and domestic animals. My work combines field sampling, laboratory experiments, and computational analyses to understand how pathogens evolve, spread, and interact with their hosts.

Over the course of my career, I have developed expertise in high-throughput sequencing technologies, genomic data analysis, and bioinformatics pipeline development. I have hands-on experience working with whole-genome sequencing datasets, identifying SNPs, indels, and understanding their role in virulence, and host adaptation.

My research is motivated by the intersection of ecology, particularly wildlife diseases, and genomics. I am particularly interested in exploring how pathogens move between wildlife and livestock, how environmental and host factors shape pathogen diversity, and how genomic tools can help predict and prevent disease outbreaks.

I am proficient in laboratory techniques including bacterial culture, DNA extraction, PCR, and Oxford Nanopore sequencing, as well as computational approaches using Python, R, and command-line tools for genome assembly, annotation, and comparative analyses. I continuously seek to expand my skills in functional genomics, transcriptomics, and advanced data visualization.

Beyond research, I enjoy mentoring students and collaborating with interdisciplinary teams to translate genomic insights into actionable strategies for disease management and conservation.

Past Projects

Anthrax and Bacillus anthracis

1. B. anthracis transmission dynamics in wildlife populations in Southern Africa, I integrate whole-genome sequence (WGS), quantitative microbiological data and ecological data that I analyzed using bioinformatics and statistical approaches to firstly determine how genetic factors and abiotic factors such as season and soil properties affect the persistence of the pathogen overtime in environmental reservoirs.

2. Using large genomic data I assessed B. anthracis genomic variation between Etosha National park and Kruger National Park, two large parks with similar wildlife host communities but different anthrax dynamics.I employ Bayesian Evolutionary Analysis Sampling Trees (BEAST) phylogeny intergrateing temporal data and host as priors (In review process with co-authors).

3. In this project I define the morphological description, host range and genomes of three novel bacteriophages of B. anthracis found in Etosha National park belonging to the family Siphoviridae. This work is highly wet-lab based involving phage progation and succeptiblity testing. (In preparation for submission).

Pneumonia in white-tailed deer

My work focused on two key objectives. First, to investigate differences in microbial communities between the nasal passages and lung tissues of deer that died from pneumonia. This work aims to enhance our understanding of disease progression and pathogenies of pneumonia in deer. Second, was to explore correlations between, pneumonia and chronic wasting disease (CWD) in relation to pathogenic microbes, while accounting for variables such as age, sex, and body condition. I applied bioinformatics and statistical methods to analyze this data. .
